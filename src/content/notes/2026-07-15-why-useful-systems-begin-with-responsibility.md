A useful system begins by taking responsibility seriously.

That sounds obvious. In practice, many systems begin somewhere else: with the software already purchased, the reporting structure already in place, or the process that is easiest to standardize. The result may look orderly while making the real work harder to see.

## Start with the responsibility

Before choosing a tool or designing a workflow, ask what responsibility the person is actually carrying.

- What outcome must they protect?
- What judgment must remain theirs?
- What information do they need at the moment of decision?
- Where does a handoff create ambiguity?

These questions shift system design away from abstract efficiency and toward practical support.

## Make the work easier to trust

Good systems do not remove judgment. They make judgment easier to exercise and easier for others to trust. They clarify what happened, what matters now, and who owns the next move.

The standard is not whether a system looks complete. The standard is whether it helps people carry responsibility with greater clarity, consistency, and care.
